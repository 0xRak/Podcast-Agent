# Fed Embraces "Run It Hot" For Powell's Final Months

**Source:** Forward Guidance (@ForwardGuidanceBW)
**Duration:** 58 minutes
**Published:** December 14, 2025 (Processed Date)

## Introduction

Recorded on the heels of a pivotal FOMC meeting, this week's Forward Guidance roundup dives deep into the Federal Reserve's apparent strategy for Jerome Powell's final months as Chair. The hosts argue that the Fed has effectively signaled a "run it hot" approach, prioritizing labor market stability and liquidity over strict inflation control as the torch is prepared to be passed. With a new $40 billion monthly reserve management operation kicking off, the stage is set for a liquidity-flush environment that is already reshaping market leadership. The discussion moves beyond just monetary mechanics, exploring how this financial repression is fueling a rotation from Big Tech to "Main Street" assets, and how suppressing market volatility is inadvertently spiking social volatility.

## Key Insights

### 1. Powell's "Coast" Strategy and the Liquidity Injection
The hosts suggest that Powell's strategy is to "coast" until his term ends, ensuring the job market looks good and inflation remains relatively contained—or at least manageable—for his successor. A key piece of evidence is the new reserve management purchase operation (roughly $40 billion/month in T-bills) slated to run specifically through April, coinciding with the transition period. This "acronym factory" move is seen as a way to offset liability increases and keep liquidity ample. The concern is that while this keeps the system oiled for now, it potentially sets up a resurgence of inflation or a "stop-start" dynamic for the next Fed Chair, creating a disjointed policy landscape in 2026.

### 2. The Great Rotation: From Mag 7 to Main Street
A significant shift in market leadership is underway. The "Mag 7" and large-cap tech stocks, which have dominated market returns, are showing signs of exhaustion or stagnation. In contrast, massive inflows are moving into small-caps (IWM), industrials, and cyclical sectors. This rotation is interpreted as a healthy broadening of the market, potentially signaling relief for the "real economy" and lower/middle-income buckets. The hosts argue that if the Fed is indeed "printing" to support the economy, the beneficiaries this time might be the more credit-dependent, tangible sectors rather than just the digital giants.

### 3. Suppressed Market Volatility Breeds Social Volatility
Profound insight is offered regarding the sociology of the current economic environment. The hosts argue that by artificially suppressing financial volatility (keeping VIX low, credit spreads tight, and asset prices high via liquidity), policymakers are inadvertently forcing that volatility to manifest socially. The widening wealth gap—where Boomers holding assets see record gains while younger generations struggle with affordability—is fueling extreme political polarization and social unrest. The "transmutation" of volatility means that if it isn't allowed to clear in the markets (via corrections or recessions), it will clear in society through instability and radicalization.

### 4. China's Manufacturing Dominance vs. US Policy
Despite aggressive rhetoric and tariffs aimed at bringing manufacturing back to the US, global trade data suggests a complex reality. China's export growth in certain sectors remains robust, simply pivoting markets to Africa, Latin America, and the EU. The US manufacturing base still struggles to compete, and the "AI capex boom" hasn't yet translated into a broad-based industrial renaissance domestically. This structural imbalance remains a critical headwind for genuine long-term US growth, regardless of short-term liquidity boosts.

## Actionable Takeaways

1.  **Rotate into Real Assets**: positioning should favor "hard assets" like gold, silver, copper, and energy. These sectors are expected to outperform in a "run it hot" inflationary environment where the dollar is being debased.
2.  **Long Small Caps / Cyclicals**: Look at the IWM (Russell 2000) and industrial sectors. The rotation out of large-cap tech suggests these "Main Street" proxies have more room to run as liquidity spreads.
3.  **Caution on Big Tech**: Consider being underweight or even hedging against "Mag 7" and semiconductor stocks, which may face headwinds from valuation ceilings and capital rotation.
4.  **Watch the "Fire Alarm"**: Monitor the correlation of rising bond yields, falling equities, and a falling dollar. If this trifecta occurs, it signals capital flight and a potential loss of faith in the "run it hot" narrative.

## Conclusion

The latest Fed moves suggest a commitment to keeping the economic engine revving high through the upcoming political and leadership transitions. While this provides a short-term tailwind for risk assets—specifically those outside the crowded tech trade—it comes with long-term risks. The "financial repression" required to maintain this state is deepening social fissures and potentially priming the pump for a second wave of inflation. For investors, the playbook is shifting: yesterday's winners (Big Tech) may not be tomorrow's leaders, and owning tangible, hard assets becomes a crucial hedge against both monetary debasement and the social volatility it creates.
